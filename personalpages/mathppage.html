Argue in favor of the view that mathematical objects exist independently of the human mind.
Edited Jun 2024

Mathematics has a way of showing up in a lot of learning subjects. I think that mathematical objects exist independently of the mind because objects exist independently of the mind and objects can represent a patterned quantity in the world that we live in. Objects are present in the real world with or without our perception, meaning that we are only subjectively viewing an objective truth of our environment and world. Objects are around and existent and so objects exist independently of the human mind. Objects are only translated into different languages, but they still symbolize the same objective truth. Objects can be deduced into sets. A biological application example is Taxonomy or the study of classifying objects concerning biodiversity and their characteristics.

Mathematics is a deduction of quantifying sets and defining objects in an environment. The hypothetical environments in mathematics are known as sets. A set is a collection of objects in which an objective environment has a pattern of association, like similarity within objects. For example, if you come across a blue pebble while walking, and then you see that there is another blue pebble along the road, and then you spot another blue pebble at the park. On the way home you spot a blue bird outside. Someone could be excited to notice that you saw this blue object of repentance in the world. Another person could be excited about the repentance of blue living things and non-living things. Imagine the perceiver sees another bluebird during a parade. The human mind does not give sets (or objects) quantities, but the existence of patterns in objects around us, combined with a subjective perspective creates a logical representation of what objects we are observing.

Another example is geometry. Natural objects demonstrate different forms of mathematical symmetry. In biology, there exists a term for bilateral symmetry and radial symmetry, which means that a biological organism grows and has two eyes, two legs, two hands, two parts of the brain, and two kidneys. Humans are classified as having bilateral symmetry. The other type of symmetry is radial. The organism grows around a central axis. A common example of this is a starfish. What does geometry have to do with symmetry? Suppose you wanted to find the perfect pay to have a barrier around your house with not four walls, but three walls of the same size. When the height of the equilateral triangle described is derived from an equilateral triangle there is a conclusion that the triangle is a sum of two isosceles triangles that have symmetry properties. Mathematics exists independently of the mind because objects exist independently, and patterns of association exist independently.

    • NCTM Math Process Standards

    Problem Solving
    Aug 2022 - May 2024

  • Build new mathematical knowledge through problem solving
  • Solve problems that arise in mathematics and in other contexts
  • Apply and adapt a variety of appropriate strategies to solve problems
  • Monitor and reflect on the process of mathematical problem solving

    • Reasoning and Proof
    Aug 2020 - May 2024

  • Recognize reasoning and proof as fundamental aspects of mathematics
  • Make and investigate mathematical conjectures
  • Develop and evaluate mathematical arguments and proofs
  • Select and use various types of reasoning and methods of proof

    • Communication
    May 2022 - May 2024

  • Organize and consolidate their mathematical thinking through communication
  • Communicate their mathematical thinking coherently and clearly to peers, teachers, and others
  • Analyze and evaluate the mathematical thinking and strategies of others;
  • Use the language of mathematics to express mathematical ideas precisely.

    • Connections
    Fall 2020 - Spring 2021 - Spring 2024

  • Recognize and use connections among mathematical ideas
  • Understand how mathematical ideas interconnect and build on one another to produce a coherent whole
  • Recognize and apply mathematics in contexts outside of mathematics

    • Representations
    Fall 2020 - Spring 2021 - Spring 2024

  • Create and use representations to organize, record, and communicate mathematical ideas
  • Select, apply, and translate among mathematical representations to solve problems
  • Use representations to model and interpret physical, social, and mathematical phenomena
